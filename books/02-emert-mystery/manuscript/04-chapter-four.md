# Chapter Four

**Monday, February 4, 2013**

Matt woke at six, showered, and was dressed by six-thirty. He met Brandon in the lobby at seven for the hotel breakfast—coffee, scrambled eggs that tasted like cardboard, toast that was either burnt or soggy depending on how long you left it in the industrial toaster.

"Sleep okay?" Matt asked.

"Not really. You?"

"About the same."

They ate quickly and drove to the Omaha Police Department. The Monday morning traffic was heavier than Friday had been—commuters heading to work, salt trucks making their rounds on icy streets. The sky was the same flat gray it had been all weekend.

Morrison was waiting for them in the conference room, folders spread across the table. She had coffee brewing in the corner and looked like she'd been there awhile.

"Morning," she said. "Got your list."

Matt poured coffee for himself and Brandon, then sat down. Morrison slid a folder across to him.

"Seventeen agents," she said. "Male, age thirty-five to fifty, licensed in Douglas, Sarpy, or Pottawattamie counties. Names similar to Michael Palmer—variations on Mike, Michael, Mitch, plus last names starting with P or similar sounds."

Matt opened the folder. Each agent had a single-page summary: photo, contact information, employment history, any complaints or legal issues flagged.

"Five of these have red flags," Morrison continued. She pointed to tabs marking specific pages. "Michael Paxton at Remax had a client threaten legal action over a commission dispute. Marcus Palmer—no relation to our victim—was sued by a buyer who claimed he misrepresented property condition. Mitchell Preston at Berkshire Hathaway has multiple complaints about aggressive sales tactics. Martin Pellman had a messy divorce two years ago with allegations of financial misconduct. Miles Peterson was investigated by the state real estate board for potential fraud, but nothing was proven."

Brandon leaned over to look at the pages. "Any of these seem like viable targets for a professional hit?"

Morrison shook her head. "Honestly? No. These are civil disputes, unhappy customers, personal problems. Nothing that screams 'hire a hitman.' But we should check them out anyway."

"Agreed," Matt said. He studied the photos. Several of the agents could pass for Palmer's brothers—same age range, similar build, comparable features. "Let's start with the five red-flagged ones. Can you set up interviews?"

"Already done. Paxton can see us at nine. Preston at eleven. Peterson at two. Marcus Palmer wasn't available today but can do tomorrow morning. Pellman's out of town until Wednesday."

Matt checked his watch. "Nine gives us time to review these files more carefully. Brandon, you want to split them?"

"Sure. I'll take Paxton, Preston, and Pellman. You take Marcus Palmer and Peterson."

They spent the next hour reading through backgrounds, looking for anything that might connect to Steven or suggest a motive for murder. Matt made notes on each agent, listing potential angles to explore in interviews.

At eight-forty-five, they headed out. Morrison drove, Matt rode shotgun, Brandon in the back. Paxton's office was in west Omaha, about twenty minutes from the station.

"What's your read on the mistaken identity theory?" Morrison asked as they drove.

Matt considered. "It's plausible. Common name, similar profession, same market. If Steven was working from limited information, he could have picked the wrong target."

"But?"

"But the planning bothers me. Someone who cases the mall, chooses an isolated property, stages the crime scene that carefully—that's not someone who makes a mistake about the target."

"So either the theory is wrong, or Steven's employer gave him bad intel."

"Right. Which means even if we find the real target, we still need to figure out who hired Steven and why."

They pulled into the parking lot of a strip mall anchored by a Walgreens. Paxton's office was sandwiched between a tax preparation service and a nail salon. The sign read "Remax Realty" in red and blue letters.

Inside, a receptionist directed them to a back office. Michael Paxton stood when they entered—mid-forties, thinning hair, slight gut pushing against his dress shirt. He shook hands nervously.

"Agents, Detective. Please, sit. Can I get you coffee?"

"We're fine," Matt said. They sat across from Paxton's desk. "Mr. Paxton, we're investigating the murder of Michael Palmer. We understand you had some issues with a client recently."

Paxton's face fell. "God, I heard about Mike. That's terrible. We weren't close, but everyone in the Omaha real estate community knows everyone else. He was a good guy."

"Tell us about your client dispute."

"That." Paxton sighed. "Look, it was a misunderstanding. My client thought he was entitled to a full commission refund because the house had some foundation issues that came up during inspection. I explained that's not how it works—inspections happen for a reason, and buyers can back out if they're not satisfied. He got angry, threatened to sue, but his lawyer told him he didn't have a case. It all blew over."

"Did he threaten you personally?"

"He said some angry things over email. 'You'll regret this,' 'I'll make sure everyone knows what kind of agent you are.' But nothing physical. And that was six months ago. I haven't heard from him since."

Matt made notes. "What's his name?"

"Derek Chen. Works in IT, lives in Bellevue."

"Do you know where he was on January ninth?"

Paxton blinked. "No idea. But I can't imagine Derek hiring someone to kill me. He was just a frustrated buyer who didn't get his way."

Brandon spoke up. "Have you had any other problems recently? Angry clients, disputes with colleagues, personal issues?"

"Nothing out of the ordinary. Real estate is full of people who aren't happy—deals fall through, houses don't sell, buyers change their minds. But it's all normal business stuff."

They asked a few more questions, but Paxton clearly wasn't the target. Matt thanked him and they left.

In the car, Morrison pulled up Derek Chen's information on her laptop. "No criminal record. Employment checks out. Social media shows him posting regularly through January, including the ninth—he was at his kid's basketball game."

"Cross him off," Matt said.

They drove to Preston's office next. Berkshire Hathaway, downtown location, nicer digs than Paxton's strip mall setup. Preston was smoother too—better suit, practiced smile, firm handshake. He listened to their questions with professional courtesy but offered nothing useful.

"Aggressive sales tactics?" Preston laughed. "That's what unsuccessful agents call it when someone closes more deals than they do. Yes, I follow up with clients multiple times. Yes, I push them to make decisions. That's how you succeed in this business. Have I made enemies? Probably. But nobody's hiring hitmen over lost commissions."

Matt had to agree. Preston was obnoxious but not murder-target material.

By noon, they'd struck out on the first two interviews. They grabbed sandwiches from a deli and ate in Morrison's car while reviewing the afternoon schedule.

"Peterson at two," Morrison said. "That leaves us time to hit one more angle. What do you want to pursue?"

Matt looked at Brandon. "What do you think? Keep working the agent list, or pivot to Palmer's background?"

"I spent the weekend on Palmer's background and got nothing. Maybe we should finish the agent list first. At least rule it out definitively."

"Makes sense," Matt said. "Morrison, can you pull Creighton records for Palmer while we handle Peterson? We'll need those eventually."

"Sure. What years?"

"1988 through 1992. Focus on anyone who overlapped with Palmer—same classes, same activities, same social circles."

Morrison made a note. "I'll call over there after we finish with Peterson."

---

Miles Peterson's office was in west Omaha, near where Palmer's firm operated. Peterson was younger than the others—mid-forties, fit, dressed casually in khakis and a polo shirt. He seemed genuinely shaken when they explained why they were there.

"Mike Palmer," Peterson said, shaking his head. "I didn't know him well, but we'd run into each other at networking events. Good reputation. I can't believe someone killed him."

"Tell us about the fraud investigation," Matt said.

Peterson grimaced. "That was blown way out of proportion. I sold a property to an investor who claimed I'd inflated the projected rental income. The real estate board investigated, found that my projections were based on market data, and closed the case. No fraud, no misconduct. Just a buyer who made a bad investment and wanted someone to blame."

"Did the buyer threaten you?"

"He filed a formal complaint. That's it. Look, I understand why you're asking—maybe someone wanted to kill me and got Mike instead. But honestly, I don't have enemies. I run a clean business, I treat clients fairly, and I don't screw people over."

Matt believed him. Peterson was straightforward, no defensive posturing, no evasion. Just another real estate agent trying to make a living.

They wrapped up the interview and headed back to the station. In Morrison's office, Matt pulled out his notebook and reviewed his notes from all three interviews.

"None of them are viable targets," he said.

"Agreed," Morrison said. "And honestly, the other agents on the list are even less likely. No complaints, no legal issues, nothing that would motivate a professional hit."

Brandon was staring out the window at downtown Omaha. "So the mistaken identity theory is probably wrong."

"Probably," Matt said. "Which brings us back to Palmer. Someone wanted him dead specifically. We need to figure out why."

Morrison pulled up her computer. "I called Creighton while you were with Peterson. Records department is pulling everything from Palmer's years there. Should have it tomorrow morning."

"Good. We also need to talk to Palmer's business partner. David Rodriguez. Get his take on whether Palmer had any secrets."

"I can set that up for this afternoon," Morrison said. "He's local."

Matt checked his watch. Two-forty-five. "Let's do it. And while we're building out Palmer's background, we should interview his golf buddies. The guys from his annual weekend. They knew him for twenty years—maybe one of them remembers something Jennifer didn't know about."

Morrison made calls while Matt and Brandon reviewed Palmer's file again. There had to be something. Some angle they'd missed. Some detail that would make sense of Steven's elaborate planning.

At three-thirty, they drove to Palmer-Rodriguez Real Estate. The office was in a converted house in the Dundee neighborhood—charming, professional, understated. A receptionist directed them to Rodriguez's office on the second floor.

David Rodriguez was late forties, Hispanic, wearing a suit that looked expensive but lived-in. His office had photos on the walls—commercial properties, ribbon cuttings, handshake deals. He stood when they entered, but his expression was guarded.

"Agents, Detective. Jennifer said you'd probably want to talk to me."

They sat. Morrison pulled out her notebook. Matt took the lead.

"Mr. Rodriguez, how long did you know Mike Palmer?"

"Twenty years. We met when we were both working for other firms. Hit it off, decided to start our own company. That was 2003."

"Tell us about him. What kind of person was he?"

Rodriguez was quiet for a moment. "Mike was... steady. Reliable. If he said he'd do something, he did it. If he made a commitment, he kept it. In twenty years, we never had a serious disagreement. Not about money, not about clients, not about anything."

"That's unusual for business partners," Matt said.

"It is. But Mike was easy to work with. He did residential, I do commercial. We each had our lanes. And we trusted each other completely."

"Did he have any problems recently? Angry clients, disputed deals, anything that might have created enemies?"

"No. Mike's clients loved him. He treated them like family. Even when deals fell through, people respected how he handled it."

Brandon leaned forward. "What about his personal life? Did he talk about problems at home, issues with friends, anything like that?"

"Mike didn't really talk about personal stuff. He mentioned Jennifer sometimes, mentioned golf with his buddies. But he kept work and personal separate. That's how he was."

"Did he seem worried about anything in the weeks before his death?"

Rodriguez considered. "No. He was normal. Maybe a little tired—January's always slow, and he was working on a big listing for February. But nothing unusual."

Matt asked more questions, probing for anything that might suggest motive. Rodriguez answered everything directly, but had nothing useful to offer. Palmer had been exactly what everyone said: a normal, successful, boring man.

After they left, Matt felt the frustration building. "We're missing something."

"Palmer's past," Brandon said. "College, early career, relationships before Jennifer. That's where the answer is."

"Maybe," Matt said. "But we need more than maybes. We need actual leads."

---

Back at the station, Morrison had arranged interviews with Palmer's golf buddies for the next morning. Matt made calls while she coordinated schedules. By six PM, they had a plan: interview the remaining similar-named agents on Tuesday, talk to Palmer's college friends, review Creighton records when they came in.

But Matt couldn't shake the feeling they were spinning their wheels. Mistaken identity was looking less likely by the hour. Palmer's background was clean. Steven had planned meticulously and disappeared completely.

"You should go back to the crime scene," Matt said to Brandon.

They were in the conference room, files spread across the table, Morrison reviewing something on her laptop. Brandon looked up.

"What?"

"The Whitmore property. You spent fifteen minutes there on Friday. Maybe you need more time. Maybe you'll pick up something clearer."

Brandon hesitated. "I don't know if it'll help."

"Your impression gave us the mistaken identity theory. Even if that's wrong, it was a starting point. Maybe there's more."

"Matt's right," Morrison said. "We're not making progress on traditional angles. If your process can give us anything—another detail, another direction—it's worth trying."

Brandon nodded slowly. "All right. Tomorrow after the interviews?"

"Yeah. I'll handle the golf buddies, you go back to the scene. We'll compare notes after."

They packed up and headed to their cars. The evening had turned colder, wind cutting through the parking lot. Matt's breath fogged as he walked.

In the car, Matt sat for a moment before starting the engine. They were four days into this investigation with nothing to show for it. No viable suspects. No clear motive. No way to find Steven.

But they'd been in this position before. Cases that seemed impossible until they weren't. The Carpenter case had felt like this at first—too many suspects, too little evidence, no clear direction. Then Brandon's impressions had pointed them toward law enforcement, and Matt's methodical work had traced everything back to Deputy Kowalski.

This case would break the same way. Some detail would shake loose. Some interview would reveal a connection. Some piece of Palmer's past would make sense of his present.

They just had to keep working. Keep pushing. Keep following every thread until one of them led somewhere.

Matt started the engine and drove back to the hotel. Tomorrow they'd interview more people, review more records, chase more leads. Eventually, something would break.

It always did.
